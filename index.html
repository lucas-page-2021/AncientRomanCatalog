Palla

Outdoor mantle for Roman women

The palla was a rectangular wrap or mantle worn over a tunic or stola when a woman went out in public. It could be draped over the shoulders, wrapped around the body, or pulled over the head.

Estimated price: 500 to 7,500 denarii (2,000 to 30,000 sestertii)

Estimate based on late-Roman clothing scholarship cited in Harlow (via Women in Antiquity); cheaper plain wraps sat at the low end, while dyed or patterned pallae were far costlier.

Stola

Long dress associated with the Roman matron

The stola was a long overgarment worn above the tunic and became a visual marker of respectable, married Roman womanhood. It was usually sleeveless or strap-suspended and fell in long folds.

Estimated price: 7,000 to 11,000 denarii (28,000 to 44,000 sestertii)

This is a late-imperial estimate inferred from published clothing price ranges for women’s garments; luxury fabrics and purple dye could push the cost much higher.

Shoes

Leather calcei and other tied Roman footwear

Closed leather shoes, often called calcei, were worn outdoors by citizens and paired with formal dress. They covered the foot and ankle and were secured with crossed leather laces.

Estimated price: 60 to 120 denarii (240 to 480 sestertii)

Based directly on Diocletian’s Price Edict entries for men’s, women’s, and higher-grade sandals or shoes in 301 CE.

Toga

Formal wool garment of the Roman citizen

The toga was a large draped outer garment worn for civic, ceremonial, and public life. It was heavy, highly structured, and increasingly associated with status and official Roman identity.

Estimated price: 1,000 to 15,000 denarii (4,000 to 60,000 sestertii)

Inference from late Roman price ceilings for wool outerwear and dyed garments in Diocletian’s edict; a plain toga would be far below a purple-striped luxury example.

Tunica

The everyday base garment of Roman dress

The tunica was the standard Roman under- and everyday garment for men and women. It was usually a simple woven piece belted at the waist, with length, color, and stripes signaling status.

Estimated price: 75 to 200 denarii (300 to 800 sestertii)

Estimated from Diocletian-era textile, tailoring, and finishing costs. A basic wool tunic was much cheaper than elite women’s dress or richly dyed ceremonial clothing.

Jewelry

Adornment in gold, gems, glass, and worked metal

Roman jewelry ranged from modest rings and bracelets to elite gold necklaces and coin-set pieces. It signaled wealth, fashion, identity, and sometimes protection or marital status.

Estimated price: 2,000 to 30,000 denarii (8,000 to 120,000 sestertii)

Broad estimate inferred from Diocletian’s gold and luxury-material prices. Small pieces in base metals could be cheaper; worked gold jewelry quickly became elite luxury.

Historical Notes

These prices are best treated as late-Roman estimates, not universal empire-wide price tags. Roman clothing costs changed by period, material, dye, and status.
